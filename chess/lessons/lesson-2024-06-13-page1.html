King Safety

The fianchetto without bishop

White plays and wins. The mating net here is having the white queen on f6, showing that a black knight on g7 is not a good defender: if the knight doesn't move we take it with mate, and if it does then it can't defend h8, thus Qh8# mate follows.

Because the f-pawn is pinned, an important detail, black is unable to defend his king under this threat; since f7-f6 is off the table (which would use the rook on f8 to defend f6, block the long diagonal (a1-h8) and allow the queen to defend g7) now white would win with the queen anywhere on that long diagonal! Nevertheless, all but f6 is defended at the moment, so which is the correct move for white to attack it? He can attack f6 with the queen in four different ways: 1. Qf3, 1. Qf2, 1. Qh4 and 1. Qg5.

Lets look at each move in turn. After 1. Qf3 black can defend f6 with 1. ... Qe7 (1. ... Ne8 still loses to the brilliant move 2. a4!! and black has to give up a piece because 2. ... Nd6 gives white a square on the long diagonal: 3. Qc3! and mate in two follows.) Here white can attempt to lure the black queen away with a rook sacrifice: 2. Rd7!? Qxd7 3. Qf6 but unfortunately now black has a check 3. ... Qd1+ 4. Bf1 Qxf1! (only move) 5. Qxf1 Re8 6. Kg1 (6. Qf6?? Re1+) 6. ... Nf5 7. g4 Nfd4 blocking the bishop and white is only slightly better.

After 1. Qf2 black must play 1. ... Nf5 right away (1. ... Qe7? isn't good enough anymore as white now has 2. Re1 to attack the queen. For example, 2. ... Qd8 3. Ba1!! (makes room on the long diagonal for the queen) 3. ... Nf5 (3. ... Nd6 4. Qb2! Nde8 5. h3 (threatens Rxe8) and black has to sacrifice the knight with 5. ... Nf6.) 4. g4 Nfd4 5. Re4 attacking the knight three times and black has to give up the piece (for example if 5. ... Ne6?? 6. Rxe6! Nc7 7. Re1). 2. g4 Qe7! 3. Re1 (3. gxf5?? Qe4+ 4. Qg2 Qxc4 and black should be winning having two extra pawns) 3. ... Qh4!= because white is forced to exchange queens or black is better (according to the engine).
